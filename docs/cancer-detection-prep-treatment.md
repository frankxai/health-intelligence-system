# Cancer Detection Prep and Treatment Decision Support

**Evidence checked:** 2026-06-15  
**Scope:** decision support and appointment preparation, not medical advice.

## Safety Boundary

This document does not diagnose cancer, rule out cancer, interpret tests, or recommend treatment. It organizes what to ask, what to track, and what to bring to clinicians.

Call emergency services or urgent care for severe or rapidly worsening symptoms. Contact the treating oncology team promptly for fever during chemotherapy, uncontrolled pain, shortness of breath, chest pain, confusion, severe dehydration, uncontrolled vomiting, heavy bleeding, or any severe side effect instructions listed by the care team.

## The Care Phase Model

Cancer work must not collapse into one vague "detection and treatment" bucket. Use six phases:

1. **Prevention & risk:** family history, genetics questions, exposure history, tobacco history, prior abnormal results.
2. **Screening:** tests for people without symptoms, usually based on age and risk.
3. **Diagnostic follow-up:** clinician-directed follow-up after symptoms or abnormal screening.
4. **Diagnosis and staging:** pathology, imaging, staging, biomarkers, and baseline health assessment.
5. **Treatment decision:** options, goals, side effects, logistics, trials, second opinion.
6. **Follow-up and survivorship:** monitoring, late effects, recurrence questions, records.

## Screening Is Not Diagnosis

Screening checks for cancer or precancer before symptoms. A positive or abnormal screening result is not automatically cancer; it means follow-up is needed. Symptoms are not "screening" and should be discussed with a clinician.

CDC supports screening for breast, cervical, colorectal, and lung cancers according to USPSTF recommendations. USPSTF recommendations apply to defined populations and often exclude people with symptoms, high-risk genetic syndromes, prior cancer, prior high-grade lesions, or other special risk factors.

## Average-Risk US Screening Snapshot

Use this table as a conversation prep baseline only.

| Cancer area | Average-risk public guidance baseline | Prep questions |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Breast | USPSTF recommends mammography every 2 years for women ages 40 to 74. Evidence is insufficient for routine screening mammography at 75+. | Do my personal risk factors change timing or modality? Do dense breasts, family history, prior biopsies, or genetic risk change the plan? |
| Cervical | USPSTF recommends ages 21-29 cytology every 3 years; ages 30-65 cytology every 3 years, high-risk HPV testing every 5 years, or cotesting every 5 years. Do not use this average-risk rule for people with prior high-grade lesions, cervical cancer, DES exposure, or compromised immunity. | Which strategy is best for me? Have I had adequate prior screening? What follow-up is needed for any abnormal result? |
| Colorectal | USPSTF recommends screening adults 45-75; ages 76-85 are individualized. Accepted strategies include stool tests, CT colonography, sigmoidoscopy, and colonoscopy at different intervals. | Which test fits my risk, preferences, access, and follow-up ability? If a stool test is positive, is colonoscopy planned? |
| Lung | USPSTF recommends annual low-dose CT for adults 50-80 with at least 20 pack-years who currently smoke or quit within 15 years; stop after 15 quit-years or if health limits curative surgery. | What is my pack-year history? Am I eligible? What are false-positive and follow-up risks? What cessation support is available? |
| Prostate | USPSTF says PSA-based screening ages 55-69 is an individual decision after discussion of benefits and harms; do not routinely screen men 70+. | What are my risk factors? What harms matter to me? What happens after an elevated PSA? |
| Skin | USPSTF finds insufficient evidence to recommend for or against routine clinician visual screening in asymptomatic adolescents and adults. | Given my risk factors and skin type, should my clinician examine specific lesions or refer dermatology? |

## Cancer Detection Prep Protocol

Use this before a primary care or screening appointment.

1. **List prior screenings:** test type, date, result label, recommended interval, missing records.
2. **List risk factors:** family history, known genetic variants, tobacco pack-years, occupational exposures, prior radiation, immunosuppression, prior abnormal tests.
3. **List symptoms separately:** symptoms require clinical evaluation and should not be processed as screening.
4. **Ask what applies:** average-risk guideline, high-risk pathway, or diagnostic pathway.
5. **Confirm follow-up plan:** who calls, when results arrive, what result triggers what next step.
6. **Record evidence date:** guideline checked date and source.

## Abnormal Result Prep

If a screening result, imaging report, lab, pathology note, or exam is abnormal:

- Do not self-stage.
- Do not ask an AI to interpret whether it is cancer.
- Ask the ordering clinician what the result means, what follow-up is recommended, and how urgent it is.
- Request copies of the report, images if relevant, pathology if relevant, and any referral order.
- Build a one-page timeline with dates, tests, results, symptoms, and next appointments.

## Diagnosis and Staging Prep

NCI explains that stage describes how much cancer is present and whether it has spread; clinicians use stage and cancer type to discuss treatment and prognosis. Before treatment decisions, ask the care team to write down:

- cancer type and exact diagnosis;
- stage and what staging system is being used;
- pathology summary;
- biomarker/genetic/molecular test status, if relevant;
- imaging completed and imaging still needed;
- whether a multidisciplinary tumor board will review the case;
- treatment intent: cure, control, symptom relief, or another stated goal.

## Treatment Decision Prep

NCI lists many treatment categories, including surgery, radiation therapy, chemotherapy, immunotherapy, targeted therapy, hormone therapy, stem cell transplant, and others. Many people receive more than one type. The right option depends on cancer type, stage, biomarkers, health status, goals, and clinician judgment.

Prepare a treatment decision brief with:

- the diagnosis and stage as written by the clinician;
- the options offered;
- the intent of each option;
- expected benefits and uncertainty;
- common serious side effects and what requires urgent contact;
- effect on fertility, sexuality, work, caregiving, travel, exercise, and mental health;
- schedule and logistics;
- cost/insurance questions;
- clinical trial availability;
- second opinion plan if wanted.

The system may help compare questions and logistics. It must not choose the treatment.

## Clinical Trial Questions

NCI recommends asking what the trial is trying to do, whether it aims to cure, slow cancer, reduce symptoms, manage side effects, or test safety. Also ask:

- Am I eligible, and who determines that?
- What phase is the trial?
- What is standard care outside the trial?
- What extra visits, scans, labs, biopsies, or travel are required?
- Who pays for routine care, trial-only care, travel, lodging, and complications?
- Can I leave the trial?
- How will the team know whether it is working?

## Second Opinion Packet

For a second opinion, gather:

- pathology report and slides/block request instructions;
- imaging reports and actual image files;
- staging summary;
- biomarker/genomic results;
- operative notes, if any;
- prior treatments and dates;
- medication list, allergies, and major health conditions;
- treating team proposal;
- personal goals and constraints.

## During Treatment

Track what clinicians ask you to track:

- treatment dates and drugs/procedures/radiation fractions;
- side effects by date, severity, and action taken;
- fever or urgent-contact thresholds given by the care team;
- missed doses or delayed sessions;
- supportive medications;
- nutrition/hydration concerns;
- mental-health concerns;
- questions for the next visit.

Do not add supplements, fasting protocols, detoxes, or alternative treatments without clinician review. Some can interfere with treatment.

## Follow-Up and Survivorship

NCI follow-up guidance emphasizes asking which doctors to see, how often, what symptoms to watch, what tests are needed after treatment, possible long-term health issues, recurrence risk, and what treatment records to keep.

Maintain:

- diagnosis and stage summary;
- treatment summary;
- pathology and imaging archive;
- follow-up schedule from clinicians;
- late-effect watch list;
- recurrence symptom instructions from clinicians;
- survivorship care plan;
- primary care and oncology responsibility split.
